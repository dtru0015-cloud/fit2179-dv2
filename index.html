④ Area Burned — Major Seasons (2000–2024)

Idiom: Interactive Line Chart · Source: ABARES · Drag year slider to highlight a season

🔴 The Black Summer (2019) burned 186M ha nationally — more than all other seasons in this dataset combined.

📈 Accelerating extremes. Before 2019, no season exceeded 25M ha. Since then, two seasons have surpassed 96M ha — a structural shift, not an anomaly.
🌡️ Climate is the driver. Peak seasons align with Australia's hottest years. As temperatures rise, the gap between a bad season and a catastrophic one keeps shrinking.

Section 3 — Why Is It Getting Worse?

Hotter conditions. Longer seasons. Rising risk.

Australia is getting hotter, and fire danger seasons are extending into spring and autumn. The data shows a clear alignment between rising temperatures and worsening fire seasons — and every state's risk score is increasing.

⑤ Fire Danger Throughout the Year

Idiom: Matrix Heatmap · Average FFDI by month · Source: Bureau of Meteorology

Victoria and NSW peak in January–February. The NT's fire season runs May–September — meaning Australia faces fire risk year-round with no safe month.

⑥ Area Burned vs. Temperature Anomaly (2000–2024)

Idiom: Dual-Axis Line Chart · Sources: ABARES · Bureau of Meteorology (ACORN-SAT)

The 2019–20 Black Summer coincides with Australia's hottest year on record (+1.52°C above baseline). Warmer years consistently align with larger burned areas.

⑦ Fire Risk Score Increasing Across All States (2001–2021)

Idiom: Slope Chart · Source: Geoscience Australia / FFDI research (Clarke et al., Dowdy et al.)

Every Australian state has seen its fire risk score increase over 20 years. NSW shows the largest rise (+0.23), but even historically lower-risk states like WA, SA and Tasmania are trending upward — there is no state immune to increasing fire danger.

Section 4 — The Human & Ecological Cost

The damage goes far beyond statistics.

Behind the hectares and temperature readings are real losses — lives ended, homes destroyed, communities uprooted, and ecosystems devastated. The 2019–20 Black Summer alone affected nearly 3 billion animals.

⑧ Deadliest Bushfire Events

Idiom: Ranked Lollipop Chart · Sources: CFA, NSW RFS, QFES, etc.

Black Saturday (2009) remains Australia's deadliest single bushfire disaster with 173 lives lost — more than all other recorded events combined.

⑧ Homes Destroyed by Major Bushfires

Idiom: Horizontal Bar Chart · Sources: CFA, NSW RFS, QFES, etc.

The Black Summer (2019–20) caused the greatest property destruction — 3,000+ homes across multiple states, surpassing even Ash Wednesday (1983).

⑨ Wildlife Impact — Black Summer 2019–20

Idiom: Treemap · Source: WWF Australia / University of Sydney (2020)

Reptiles dominated the toll (2.46B — 81% of all affected animals), followed by birds (180M), mammals (143M) and frogs (51M). One of the worst wildlife disasters in modern history.

⑩ Cumulative Economic Losses — A Step Change in Scale

Idiom: Waterfall Chart · Source: Insurance Council of Australia

Each bar shows the economic loss added by a major bushfire event. The Black Summer's $103B single-season loss is larger than all previous events across 80 years combined — a single catastrophic step change in Australia's bushfire costs.

Section 5 — Future Vulnerability

More Australians may be in harm's way.

As Australia's population grows and spreads into fire-prone areas, the number of people directly exposed to high fire risk is increasing — even as fire seasons grow more intense and frequent.

⑪ Population Exposed to High Bushfire Risk (2001–2021)

Idiom: Dot Matrix / Bubble Plot · Sources: ABS Census (2001–2021) & Geoscience Australia

NSW consistently has the largest exposed population, growing from 1.24M in 2001 to 1.85M in 2021 — a 49% increase. Every state shows upward growth, meaning millions more Australians now live within high fire risk zones than at the start of this century.

So — Are We Becoming More Vulnerable?

🔥 Seasons are worsening — The Black Summer burned 186M ha. Three of the four worst seasons on record occurred after 2019.
🌡️ Conditions are deteriorating — Every state's fire risk score has risen since 2001. Australia's hottest years align with its worst fire seasons.
🐾 Ecology is under threat — Nearly 3 billion animals were harmed in a single season. The Black Summer was one of the worst wildlife disasters in modern history.
💸 Costs are escalating — Economic losses have grown from millions to hundreds of billions. The Black Summer alone cost an estimated $103B.
👥 Exposure is growing — Hundreds of thousands more Australians now live in high fire risk zones than in 2001.
⚠️ The evidence is clear — Hotter conditions, more severe seasons, rising risk scores, and growing population exposure all point in the same direction.

The evidence suggests Australia is becoming more vulnerable.

Bushfires are not new to Australia. But the combination of extreme fire weather, escalating human and ecological losses, and growing population exposure demands urgent attention.
