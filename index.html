Australia's Bushfire Crisis:
Are We Becoming More Vulnerable?

Exploring the trends, impacts, and growing risks of bushfires across Australia — from state-by-state exposure to the human cost of our most devastating seasons.

Bushfires are part of Australia's history.
But the scale and intensity of recent seasons —
and the number of people in harm's way —
are changing.

more vulnerable.

① Bushfire Risk Across Australia

Where does fire danger concentrate?

Australia's bushfire risk is not evenly distributed. New South Wales, Victoria, and the ACT consistently face the highest combined risk — driven by their dense vegetation, population pressure, and exposure to extreme fire weather.

② The Pattern

This is not random.

Looking across decades, certain states repeatedly bear the heaviest burden. The rankings shift, but the vulnerability persists — and worsens with each extreme season.

⑤ State Ranking by Decade — Direct Deaths

Source: AFAC / Various historical records

NSW has risen to rank 1 in the 2010s and 2020s, overtaking Victoria which dominated in the 2000s — driven by Black Saturday (2009) and the Black Summer (2019–20).

⑥ Area Burned vs. Temperature Anomaly (2000–2024)

Sources: Geoscience Australia / ABARES · Bureau of Meteorology (ACORN-SAT)

The 2019–20 Black Summer coincides with Australia's hottest year on record (+1.52°C above baseline). Warmer conditions align with — though do not solely cause — larger burned areas.

③ The Explanation

More fire. Hotter times.

Australia's warming trend is clear — and it aligns with increasingly severe fire seasons. As temperatures rise, conditions become more dangerous, and the fire season extends further into spring and autumn.

⑦ Fire Danger Throughout the Year

Average Fire Danger Index (FFDI) · Source: Bureau of Meteorology

Victoria and NSW peak in January–February. The Northern Territory's fire season runs May–September — almost opposite to the south — meaning Australia faces fire risk year-round.

⑧ Deaths and Homes Destroyed — Major Events

Sources: CFA, NSW RFS, QFES, etc.

Black Saturday (2009) remains the deadliest single event with 173 lives lost. The Black Summer (2019–20) caused the most property destruction — 3,000+ homes across multiple states.

④ The Human Cost

The damage is personal.

Behind the hectares and indices are real losses — lives ended, homes destroyed, and communities uprooted. The scale of individual events reveals just how devastating Australian bushfires can be.

⑨ Economic Losses Are Escalating

Major bushfires (1939–2023) · Source: Insurance Council of Australia

The Black Summer's estimated $103B economic loss dwarfs all previous events combined. Bubble size represents direct deaths — showing that the largest economic impacts also carry high human costs.

⑩ Population Living in High Bushfire Risk Areas

Sources: ABS (2001, 2006, 2011, 2016, 2021) & Geoscience Australia

Since 2001, the number of Australians living in high fire risk zones has grown by over 600,000. NSW alone has seen a jump from 18.8% to nearly 23% of its population at risk.

⑤ The Future

More Australians may be in harm's way.

As Australia's population grows and spreads into fire-prone areas, the number of people directly exposed to high fire risk is increasing — even as fire seasons grow more intense.

⑪ Risk is Spreading: Change in Fire Risk Score (2001–2021)

Source: Geoscience Australia

Every state has seen its fire risk score increase over 20 years. NSW shows the largest rise (+0.23), while even historically lower-risk states like WA and SA are trending upward.

The data is clear:
we are becoming
more vulnerable.

Bushfires are not new to Australia. But the combination of extreme fire weather, repeated severe seasons, and growing human exposure suggests our vulnerability is increasing.

The Bottom Line

🔥 Extreme fire seasons are becoming more frequent — with three of the four worst seasons occurring after 2019.
🌡️ Conditions are getting hotter — 2019 was Australia's warmest year on record, with 8 of the 9 hottest years occurring since 2013.
👥 More Australians are living in harm's way — exposure to high fire risk zones has grown in every state since 2001.

The data is clear: we are becoming more vulnerable.
